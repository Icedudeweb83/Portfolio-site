Goal

To create an effective and satisfying user interface design for a high-level design challenge.

1. User evaluation.

2. Ideate, design, prototype.

3. User test, iterate.

For this class project in User Interface Design, I was tasked with solving how a UX Design student at Austin Community College can manage and navigate the onslaught of online resources available, recommended or required during their time as a student. I was also tasked with using an iterative design process to create and test interface solutions for â€‹the discovered needs, with the only constraint being to work within the technical limitations of existing devices and features.

The Challenge

Give a UX Design student an interface to locate, vet and read or interact with validated resources to further their learning and career. Students should be able to search for resources by topic, validate the credential or quality of the resource, and apply their learnings to their career goals.

Who's the User?

  • Who am I designing for?
  • What are their needs, behaviors and goals?
I began by evaluating the needs of my users. As a UX Design student myself, I drew on my own experience managing online resources (often articles). I realized I sometimes found articles myself, but primarily I received articles forwarded by professors.
My own behaviors involved locating and reading articles relevant to my courses or career topics; it also involved retaining those articles for future reference when completing course assignments, which I found particularly useful when developing new skills or building on my aptitude for performing user design work. My needs may have varied from simply satisfying course requirements to further educating myself outside of class.

Research and statement of finding

My online research revealed there are no reliable methods or guidepoints to direct a user towards consistent, quality UX resources. While quality articles exist, and there are even good collections at sites like Medium.com, extensive user research would be needed to form a detailed solution with vast coverage for UX students specifically. Since this challenge is structured for a quick deliverable, and because it's intended to aid ACC students in particular, I decided to focus my strategy on the creation of an article database procured by professors and students, along with a process of vetting/approval of new articles between professors and students based on school curriculum. This would not only make for easier design parameters, but would (theoretically) provide trustworthy assistance and context for users.

Ideate: Crazy 8's

I began ideating using Crazy 8's. This helped generate some layout ideas and features.
Ideas I chose to explore as a result include...
  • Applying a strong visual approach to organizing articles by course.
  • Applying a rating system.
  • Applying filter options.
  • Applying tags.
  • Applying toggle options to activate/deactivate features.

Ideate: Low-fidelity sketches & Silent critique

  • Low-fidelity sketches
  • Silent critique
Next I used a desktop layout to explore concepts and relationships among different features. One concept that eventually became the focus of my user test was a suggestions feature!
Feedback from classmates was especially helpful. Among the recomendations were to apply a stronger graphical approach to the pages and articles, utilizing a simple layout for appeal and accessibility; to populate articles with combined student/professor ratings or recommends; and to consider making Page 2 the home page. I decided to incorporate all of this advice as I felt it was useful.

Design: Mid-fidelity

From here I moved to a mid-fidelity digital mockup. I switched to a mobile app layout as I felt it would be more challenging to incorporate my features - and would be the more likely format for many users.

Prototype: Mid-fidelity

  • Persona
  • Task
  • Why this task?
  • Scenario
  • Anticipated user flow

As my design started to come to life and look more like a real app, I began thinking about its use. Who might use it? What might they need to do with it? So I created a persona, a task and a scenario to give my design some direction and to motivate my prototype.

With concept and features mostly set, I chose a second-year student, Julian, who is familiar with UX Design concepts as well as the school's program map. He is comfortable looking for additional outside resources and making suggestions to other students if given the opportunity.

I would like Julian to accomplish the following tasks:
  • Go to his article suggestions.
  • Locate a suggestion that was declined.
  • Determine why it was declined.
  • Go to the appropriate screen to resubmit.
Among different possible tasks based on my design, I chose this one because it would require Julian to navigate deeply to accomplish a task highly relevant to committed use of the app and its potential for building and curating a database.
Julian was really excited to submit an article on Invision to the HotZone app, but just received notification his article was declined. He was so certain it would be a great addition and really wants to know why it was turned down. How would he locate the administratorâ€™s feedback on the app? How would he address this feedback if he felt he could make a correction?
Based on the scenario, my anticipated user flow for Julian would look like this:
  1. He logs in.
  2. He taps on his avatar to go to his profile page.
  3. He taps the Suggestions tab to go to his suggestions subpage.
  4. He locates the article with the notification and taps the comment icon.
  5. He reads the administrator's comments appearing on the menu overlay and taps the "resubmit" shortcut.
  6. He arrives at the Add Suggestion page to submit again.

User Test

I user-tested my prototype with three classmates and received helpful feedback. Primarily it seemed users got to where they needed to, but there were two areas to definitely improve: communication and visuals.
  • Communication
  • Visuals
It was recommended I add some kind of alarm or alert graphic to notify users where a new message might be found, and it was also suggested I add a reply option to the administrator's comments.
Some users felt the contents were too small, suggesting better sizing by perhaps adjusting elements and proportions for a more comfortable fit and, most importantly, better usage.

Iterate: High-fidelity design and prototype

Iterating on these changes, and putting them onto a sleek smartphone template, produced this high-fidelity design/prototype, which was my final deliverable for class. Click here to try my prototype! 😎
  • Improving communication
  • Improving visuals
This became the most iterative area of my prototype! Adding an alert icon to appropriate screens was simple enough, as was adding a reply element to the administrator's comments. But the addition of that reply element led to a whole new feature to explore: the user's account! I designed and implemented a dedicated communication feature - a message center - to improve communication flow between suggestions and the approval process.
Adjusting visuals was an exercise in discretion. I found a nice balance between enlarging some elements, such as the article preview images, while simply working with space on others. This included adjusting how items take available space, such as the "from professors/students" toggle (whose adjustment allowed me to include a layout option), as well as adjusting how items use empty space, such as the more spacious, visually simpler login screen.

Reflect

The goal of this project was to create an interface that was both effective and satisfying, and I believe I have achieved precisely that. One particular UX strength of mine, in the area of design, is striking a pleasing balance between stylish visuals and practical functionality most users seem to find intuitive. My graphic design experience shaped my disciplined approach to making visuals accountable to an artistic purpose, and transferring that to a user purpose is not only exciting and rewarding, but it is one of the main reasons I have pursued a career in UX Design.
