Goal

To improve how an existing website organizes and presents information.

1. Observe the site.

2. Research the site.

3. Redesign the site.

For this class project in User Centered Design, I was asked to work both with classmates and by myself to evaluate a website dedicated to science fiction and fantasy book reviews. The task was to apply established UX principles to form a deeply detailed analysis of the site's contents and objectives, and to use that analysis to redesign the site for an improved user-centered experience.

The Challenge

Use your knowledge to analyze a real website by conducting a content audit; gather information from research, data synthesis and user testing; and, with that information, redesign the site to improve how it organizes and presents information.

The Current State

The existing website

I began by familiarizing myself with the visual layout of the current site: how the homepage is structured, what topics make up the main navigation (and what that tells me about their importance), and the grid style formatting used to present titles and information for each review (A). I also explored one of the category pages, which offered consistency in appearance and content (B). And I sampled a book review as well (C).

Conducting a Content Audit

Auditing the site was my next step. I was provided raw data from an automated crawl, and reviewed its contents for a peek "behind the curtain."
To make sense of what I was looking at, I copied the information into another spreadsheet and organized it with an entry for every page of the site. This allowed me to, first of all, contextualize what I was looking at. Second it allowed me to scope the content to determine what kind of material there was and what might be worth keeping for a closer look.

Interviewing the Stakeholder

Although this was an improvement, and revealed new components previously unseen such as Nebula and Locus award categories, I found it wasn't enough to bring meaning to what I actually saw on the site. Important details needed to come into better focus. So, together with a group of fellow students, we conducted an interview with the stakeholder. We agreed on a series of questions we felt would explore four key priorities:
  • What kind of content is it?
  • How is the site structured?
  • How effective is the content?
  • How is the content managed?
Here are our questions and answers.
As the stakeholder was also the author and designer of the site, I found his answers gave me considerable insight into his mental model. Although I needed to visit the site again to better comprehend his choices, I felt I understood his perspective on presenting this information. I was particularly struck by his answer on pain points: design and information architecture were the two biggest issues in his view. I agreed, and found our shared opinion influenced my work on the remainder of the project.

Categorizing and labeling content

Armed with my new insights, and with a view towards composing an eventual sitemap, I returned to the website and began categorizing content on Trello. By matching criteria and parameters, I adjusted my categories until I had a good feel for what made sense to me. Particularly helpful was labeling content that was not consistent across the board (in blue). This helped me see what was consistent, and therefore what should likely remain priorities in the redesign.
Now I felt like I had not only a solid understanding of the site's details, but a macro view of its contents and intentions.

The Research

Card Sort

The first step in conducting my research was discovering how potential users, unfamiliar with the site, might comprehend its contents. So together with my group we generated questions to ask in an open card sort. I settled on 30 questions I wanted to ask, or items I wanted sorted, based on how I perceived the relevance of the questions to the importance of the contents. Here are the 30 I chose:
  • Search for reviews by book author.
  • After reading a book review, see books by the same author.
  • Read most recent review.
  • See a list of reviews in order of most recent to least recent.
  • Who wrote this review?
  • What books are popular right now?
  • What are Hugo awards?
  • Read about a book that could fall into multiple genres.
  • Learn more about how the writer rates books.
  • What types of books are on this site?
  • Find a book that has been nominated for an award.
  • Highly reviewed books are important to me.
  • Understand what are the different prizes for sci-fi books.
  • Read something brand new.
  • Find a review of non-fiction books.
  • Understand all of the genres.
  • Compare books within a genre.
  • Find information about the reviewer.
  • Search for similar books to the one I just finished.
  • Identify the date that the book review was published.
  • I'm looking for a series to read.
  • Pick a book that is highly rated.
  • Search for science fiction authors.
  • Understand the star rating system.
  • Learn the difference between fantasy and sci-fi.
  • Discover new fantasy book reviews.
  • Look for a book with multiple parts.
  • I want a good novel to listen to.
  • What are people saying about this book on social media?
  • Which of these books should I read next?
Due to time constraints I was able to run my card sort with just one individual. However I was able to do so in person, observe closely and ask her thoughtful questions afterwards. Her approach seemed to be broad; specifically, she ended up creating four categories named "Awards and Prizes," "Genre," "Looking for Something to Read," and "Reviews." In talking with her, she revealed her tendency was to organize cards based on how she felt people would want to generally search, which I found interesting. I asked her a few questions about her choices:
Clearly her method of organization, though broad, reflects what many people would likely do in an open card sort: shift from a macro view to a micro view, and back, as she applied information from each view to contextualize the entire scheme in a way that made sense to her. Keeping both views in mind was essential to my eventual redesign.

Sitemap

Based on what I'd learned, I moved on to create my first sitemap.
Looking over this first iteration, it seemed award-winning reviews were nested too deeply within "Genre." The Hugo, Locus and Nebula-awarded reviews were not only too important to be so inaccessible, but I felt they were spread too thin across the two genres. I felt the "About" topics were too spread out as well, and would fit more logically within a common category. So I created a general "About" column for the About topics as well as the awarded reviews (beginning with information "about" them) so they fit more tightly.
It was this sitemap I used for an in-class silent critique as well as a tree test.

Silent Critique

This is the part of the project that really opened my eyes.
In focusing on the awards and About, and in trying to ensure links to reviews were available easily, I utterly failed to realize I had placed reviews in three different locations! I neglected to avoid the tricky habit of creating too many paths, and therefore creating too many indecisions for my users. And, indeed, that's what's reflected in my tree test.

Tree Test

I returned to Optimal Workshop to create my tree test...
... and used it to craft six tasks for my users to perform but without giving too many clues as to where I wanted them to go. These are the tasks and the initial results from my nine users.
Things clearly didn't turn out as well as I had hoped. I dug further into the results on Optimal and documented my observations.

Final Sitemap

Based on what I'd learned here, I moved on to create my new (and finalized) sitemap. The need to overhaul is certainly an understatement! My top priorities were to modify the top navigation level so topics are more evenly distributed and accessible, and to reduce the number of "Review" occurrences overall. I began by eliminating the Genres category, which removed one incidental source for reviews and kept them (mostly) limited to just the Reviews category. I then created a new top level category for Awarded Books, which placed all award-related content in one place. Then I created a new top-level category just for the site's handling of book titles: How We Review. This left the reviewer's (Suncerae's) bio as the only item in About.
The result is four top-level categories, just as before, but organized in a way that should significantly reduce cognitive load on the user:

Reflections

This was an eye-opening project for me, and one of the most informative and influential I've worked on. Its scope was honestly bigger than I had anticipated, but I love that it pushed me and I'm certain to have a stronger perspective on handling a similar project in the future. Below are my thoughts on aspects of the project that could have been better.
  • The clarity of the sitemaps
  • The language of the tasks
  • The user

The biggest lesson, hands down. I stacked too much content within the top navigation level, I created too many paths for reviews, and my users were confused. I will remember this for next time. 🚀

While I meant well, I indulged too much in trying to camouflage where I wanted my users to go. I really wanted them to not be tipped off, but when combined with the sitemap issue, it just turned into a mass of confusion. I needed to design tasks that spoke the same language as the site's users, which meant understanding who my target users are, writing from their perspective, and using plain language as much as possible.

For this project, a bigger focus on content synthesis, site navigation and design unfortunately resulted in a smaller focus on deep user engagement. While the tree test results were fantastic to analyze, I wish I had been able to access a group specifically made up of sci-fi and fantasy readers, and that I had been able to engage more users overall than I did. And I would have particularly loved to tree test my final sitemap for comparison to my earlier results.
